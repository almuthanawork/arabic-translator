# Rhetorical Device Handling Guide (البلاغة)

This reference covers how to handle each major Arabic rhetorical device when translating to English. The principle throughout: reproduce the EFFECT, not the form.

## سجع

**What it is:** Rhyming prose — clauses that end with matching sounds, creating an incantatory rhythm.

**How to handle:** Create rhythmic English prose using assonance, consonance, parallel structure, or cadence. Full rhyme replication is rarely possible. Prioritize the flowing, incantatory quality over matching end-rhymes. If the سجع creates a sense of gravity or ceremony, find English structures that feel weighty and measured.

**Example approach:** If the Arabic has three clauses with matching -ān endings creating a rolling rhythm, find English sentences with similar length and internal sound echoes, even if they don't rhyme.

## طباق

**What it is:** Antithesis — paired opposites placed in structural parallel for emphasis.

**How to handle:** English has a strong antithesis tradition. Maintain structural parallelism and preserve the sharp contrast. This is one of the easier devices to carry across.

**Example:** "He who has no past has no present." Keep the balance, keep the bite.

---

## جناس

**What it is:** Paronomasia — wordplay based on phonetic similarity between words with different meanings.

**How to handle:** Find English near-homophones or puns that capture the spirit. If impossible at that exact point, compensate with wordplay elsewhere in the passage to preserve the ludic (playful) texture. Don't force a bad pun just to match placement.

---

## تورية

**What it is:** Double entendre — a word used with both a surface meaning and a hidden intended meaning.

**How to handle:** Find an English word with analogous double meaning when possible. When impossible, preserve the primary (intended) meaning and add a [translator's note] only if the secondary meaning is essential to the passage. This is rare — most of the time, losing the secondary meaning is acceptable if the primary lands well.

---

## كناية

**What it is:** An expression that works simultaneously at the literal and figurative levels. The reader can take it either way, and the text gains richness from both readings being active at once.

**How to handle:** This is the hardest device — no systematic English equivalent exists. Seek English expressions that also admit dual readings. This is case-by-case judgment. Sometimes you'll find an English idiom that does double duty. Sometimes you'll need to accept a partial loss and compensate elsewhere. The key is awareness: don't accidentally collapse a كناية into a flat literal or a flat figurative reading. Keep the shimmer of both.

## استعارة

**What it is:** Metaphor — describing one thing in terms of another, with the comparison particle omitted.

**How to handle:** When the vehicle (image) is culturally accessible, preserve it directly. When it depends on Arabic-specific associations (desert imagery, tribal metaphors, classical poetic conventions), adjust the vehicle while preserving the tenor (the thing being described) and the emotional weight. A "condensed" Arabic metaphor can often stay compressed in English.

**Caution:** Don't substitute Western cultural images for Arabic ones. If an Arabic metaphor uses desert wind as a vehicle for grief, don't replace it with autumn rain. Find something that carries the same weight without importing a foreign landscape.

---

## تشبيه

**What it is:** Simile — explicit comparison using a particle (like كَ or مِثل).

**How to handle:** Similar to metaphor, but the comparison is overt. Preserve the vehicle when culturally accessible. When the comparison element is uniquely Arabic, adjust while keeping the emotional logic. Arabic similes sometimes omit the comparison particle (تشبيه بليغ) — these can usually stay compressed in English.

---

## المجاز المرسل

**What it is:** Metonymy — referring to something by a related attribute or associated thing (part for whole, cause for effect, container for contained, etc.)

**How to handle:** When the metonymic relationship is universal (e.g., "the crown" for the monarchy), preserve it directly. When culture-specific (e.g., "the tent" for hospitality/home), find an English metonymy with equivalent weight and resonance, or naturalize the reference with a light contextual touch.

---

## إيجاز

**What it is:** Conciseness — saying much in few words, or deliberately leaving things unsaid through grammatical inference or cultural assumption.

**How to handle:** Trust the English reader. Resist the urge to over-explain. What Arabic leaves unsaid through grammatical inference, keep unsaid in English through contextual inference when possible. Brevity is a rhetorical choice — if the author was concise, you should be too. Adding explanatory padding destroys the effect.

---

## إطناب

**What it is:** Elaboration — deliberate repetition, expansion, or amplification.

**How to handle:** Distinguish purposeful repetition (emotional escalation, rhythmic building, emphasis, incantation) from conventional elaboration (formulaic phrases added by habit or genre convention). Preserve the former faithfully — the building effect is the whole point. Reduce the latter to English norms, because conventions differ across languages and what feels natural in Arabic may feel padded in English.

---

## التفات

**What it is:** A sudden shift in grammatical person, number, or tense within a passage, used for dramatic effect. (e.g., shifting from third person to second person mid-sentence to draw the reader in.)

**How to handle:** This is powerful and intentional. Preserve the shift in English. If the Arabic suddenly addresses "you" after narrating in third person, do the same in English. The disorientation is the point — it pulls the reader into the text. Don't "fix" it into smooth consistency.
