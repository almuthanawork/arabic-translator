# Arabic Rhetorical Devices (البلاغة) — Translation Guide

Reproduce the EFFECT, not the form. When form IS the effect (as in سجع or طباق), find English forms that create the same feeling.

---

## سجع (Rhyming Prose)
Clauses ending with matching sounds, creating incantatory rhythm.
**Handle:** Use assonance, consonance, parallel structure, or cadence. Don't force end-rhymes.

**Example:** الثقة والراحة واليقين — the -a endings create a rolling rhythm. English: "confidence, comfort, and certainty" preserves the tricolon cadence without forcing rhyme.

## طباق (Antithesis)
Paired opposites in structural parallel.
**Handle:** English has a strong antithesis tradition. Maintain the balance and the bite.

**Example:** من لا ماضي له لا حاضر له → "He who has no past has no present."

## جناس (Wordplay / Paronomasia)
Phonetically similar words with different meanings.
**Handle:** Find English near-homophones or puns. If impossible here, compensate with wordplay elsewhere in the passage.

## تورية (Double Entendre)
A word with surface meaning and hidden intended meaning.
**Handle:** Find an English word with analogous double meaning. When impossible, preserve the primary meaning. Add a note only if the secondary meaning is essential.

## كناية (Simultaneous Literal/Figurative)
An expression that works at both levels simultaneously. The richness comes from both readings being active.
**Handle:** The hardest device. Seek English expressions that also admit dual readings. Don't collapse into flat literal or flat figurative. Case-by-case.

**Example:** واجهة المملكة — literally "the Kingdom's interface" AND figuratively "the Kingdom's face/representation." Use "face" to keep both readings alive.

## استعارة (Metaphor)
**Handle:** Preserve culturally accessible vehicles. When the image depends on Arabic-specific associations (desert, tribal, classical), adjust the vehicle while preserving tenor and emotional weight. Don't substitute Western images for Arabic ones.

## تشبيه (Simile)
**Handle:** Preserve the vehicle when accessible. Arabic similes sometimes omit the comparison particle (تشبيه بليغ) — these can usually stay compressed in English.

**Example:** كأنه همسٌ قادم من زمن آخر → "like a whisper carried from some other age"

## المجاز المرسل (Metonymy)
Referring to something by an associated attribute (part for whole, cause for effect).
**Handle:** Universal metonymies ("the crown" for monarchy) preserve directly. Culture-specific ones ("the tent" for home/hospitality) need English equivalents with similar weight.

## إيجاز (Conciseness / Ellipsis)
Saying much in few words, or deliberately leaving things unsaid.
**Handle:** Trust the English reader. Don't over-explain. If the author was brief, you should be too. But if a gap would confuse the target reader, embed a brief contextual cue naturally — don't add a footnote.

## إطناب (Elaboration)
Deliberate repetition or amplification.
**Handle:** Distinguish purposeful repetition (emotional escalation, rhythm) from conventional padding. Preserve the former, reduce the latter to English norms.

## التفات (Grammatical Shift)
Sudden shift in person, number, or tense for dramatic effect.
**Handle:** Preserve the shift. If Arabic switches from third person to addressing "you," do the same in English. The disorientation is intentional.

## توكيد (Emphasis Through Repetition)
Repeating a word or using إنّ / لقد / قد for emphasis.
**Handle:** Find English emphasis mechanisms: "indeed," sentence stress through word order, italics (sparingly), or repetition if it works in English.

**Example:** وَلَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا — the لقد adds emphatic certainty. "We created" is flat; "We have indeed created" or simply "We created" with the weight carried by context and cadence.

## استفهام (Rhetorical Questions)
Questions asked for effect, not information.
**Handle:** Keep them as questions. Don't convert to statements. The unanswerable quality IS the point.

**Example:** كيف تهرب من من هو أقرب إليك منك → "How do you run from the One who is closer to you than you are to yourself?"

## قسم (Oath Formulas)
Swearing by God, time, the pen, etc.
**Handle:** In Quranic/classical text, preserve the oath structure ("By the morning light..."). In casual speech, assess whether والله is a genuine oath or just emphasis (like English "I swear" or "honestly").
